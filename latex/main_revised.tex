% ---------- Abstract ----------
\begin{abstract}
This paper presents a dual-UAV framework for integrated sensing and communication with advanced physical-layer modeling. Unlike prior works that employ simplified propagation and energy models, the proposed formulation incorporates Rician fading with line-of-sight and non-line-of-sight transitions, Cram\'{e}r-Rao-bound-based bistatic sensing, secrecy-rate modeling with artificial noise and cooperative jamming, aerodynamic UAV energy consumption, and queue-aware quality-of-service modeling. Two cooperative UAVs trained using soft actor-critic jointly optimize trajectories and resource allocation across communication, sensing, security, and energy objectives. Simulation results show a 22\% improvement in energy efficiency over static heuristic baselines while maintaining a favorable trade-off between sensing accuracy and communication sum-rate. The results confirm the effectiveness of learning-based dual-UAV control under realistic physical-layer constraints.
\end{abstract}

UAV networks, Integrated Sensing And Communication(ISAC), Deep Reinforcement Learning Methods, Physical layer security, cooperative jamming.

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

UAVs have emerged as a transformative technology for next-generation wireless networks owing to their high mobility, flexible deployment, and ability to establish strong line-of-sight (LoS), air-to-ground (A2G), and ground-to-air (G2A) links~\cite{khuwaja2018survey, kurunathan2024mluav}. The emerging paradigm of Integrated Sensing and Communication (ISAC) further enables UAV platforms to support communication and environmental awareness through shared spectrum, mobility, and payload resources~\cite{wu2023uavisac, meng2024isacsurvey}. However, UAV-assisted ISAC design remains challenging because communication throughput, sensing accuracy, secrecy, and propulsion energy are tightly coupled through the UAVs' three-dimensional (3D) trajectories.

Security is particularly important in UAV-assisted networks because of the broadcast nature of wireless propagation and the LoS-dominant aerial links expose confidential data to passive eavesdroppers~\cite{pandey2022security, sun2019pls, wu2019safeguarding}. Physical layer security (PLS) techniques, including artificial noise (AN), cooperative jamming, power allocation, and trajectory optimization, have therefore been widely studied for UAV systems~\cite{wang2020energysecure, zhang2021dualuavdata, diao2022noma}. Recent dual-UAV architectures show that one UAV can operate as an information relay while another UAV operates as a friendly jammer, thereby degrading the eavesdropper link without requiring cryptographic cooperation from the destination~\cite{pandeydualuav2026, pandeyrobust2025, kang2022dualuav}. Related works also combine UAV communications with RF energy harvesting and Simultaneous Wireless Information and Power Transfer(SWIPT) to extend network lifetime~\cite{perera2018swipt, pandey2025rfeh, pandeyswiptjam2025}, while hardware-impairment and shadowing analyses highlight the need for realistic channel and transceiver models~\cite{pandeyiot2023, pandeyrfehiot2025}.

The channel and resource-allocation literature further motivates the use of detailed A2G/G2A models. Rician fading with elevation-dependent LoS probability is a common choice for UAV links~\cite{khuwaja2018survey, pandeyaoidrl2025}; secrecy-aware resource allocation has been investigated for UAV relays, cooperative jammers, and robust trajectory design~\cite{xu2021secureuav, wang2021robust3d, gao2024roleswitching}; and STAR-RIS-assisted secure communication has recently expanded the design space for full-space signal control against HPPP eavesdroppers~\cite{pandeystarris2026}. For propulsion modeling, recent UAV optimization papers adopt the rotary-wing aerodynamic power model that captures blade profile, induced, and fuselage drag components rather than relying on linear energy consumption approximations~\cite{zeng2019energy, pandeydualuav2026, pandeyaoidrl2025}.

Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) has become a powerful tool for continuous, high-dimensional UAV optimization because it can learn policies under stochastic channels and moving constraints without solving a new non-convex program in every time slot~\cite{haarnoja2018sac, bai2023marluav}. Recent examples include AoI-aware UAV-assisted networks with RF-EH~\cite{pandeyaoidrl2025}, DRL-driven STAR-RIS secure communication~\cite{pandeystarris2026}, constrained SAC for energy-aware UAV-IoT trajectories~\cite{zhou2022csac}, and multi-UAV ISAC resource allocation~\cite{zhang2023drlisac}. These studies show strong promise, but they typically emphasize only a subset of security, sensing, energy, and queue-aware service requirements.

Despite substantial progress, three critical research gaps remain. First, most UAV security works focus on secrecy-rate maximization or energy harvesting without jointly considering ISAC-style sensing and queue-aware QoS. Second, many UAV-ISAC studies adopt simplified channel, secrecy, or energy models, whereas recent Pandey-style dual-UAV designs explicitly account for RF-powered relay/jammer operation, information causality, energy causality, and inter-UAV collision avoidance~\cite{pandeydualuav2026, pandeyrobust2025}. Third, the effect of cooperative jamming and AN on a dual-UAV ISAC system with CRB-guided sensing and aerodynamic energy consumption is still insufficiently characterized.

This paper addresses these gaps through three contributions. First, we develop a dual-UAV framework that combines Rician fading~\cite{khuwaja2018survey}, CRB-based bistatic sensing~\cite{zhang2023drlisac}, secrecy-rate modeling with AN and cooperative jamming~\cite{pandeyswiptjam2025, pandeydualuav2026}, and rotary-wing aerodynamic energy consumption~\cite{zeng2019energy, pandeyaoidrl2025}. Second, we formulate the multi-objective decision problem with explicit mobility, altitude, inter-UAV separation, battery, resource-allocation, and secrecy constraints inspired by recent dual-UAV secure communication formulations~\cite{wang2021robust3d, pandeydualuav2026}. Third, through ablation studies and Pareto analysis, we demonstrate 15--20\% performance improvements over simplified baselines, validated across multiple random seeds.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:motivation} presents the motivation and research gaps. Section~\ref{sec:system} presents the system and mathematical models. Section~\ref{sec:problem} formulates the optimization problem. Section~\ref{sec:drl} describes the DRL framework. Section~\ref{sec:results} provides experimental results. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes the paper.

\section{Motivation and Research Gaps}
\label{sec:motivation}

The system considered in this paper is motivated by secure dual-UAV relay/jammer communication, where a base station transfers information to a destination node through a UAV relay while a second UAV transmits jamming signals to suppress eavesdropping. This architecture is attractive because the relay and jammer can exploit 3D mobility to improve the legitimate B--$U_R$--D path and simultaneously degrade the Eve links, as in recent dual-UAV secure communication studies~\cite{wang2021robust3d, pandeydualuav2026}. At the same time, UAV mobility also creates useful sensing geometry, so the same aerial deployment can be extended toward ISAC-style operation when the destination node represents the monitored target or scheduled IoT device.

Recent research available on this usually usually optimize either secure relaying and cooperative jamming~\cite{pandeyswiptjam2025, pandeydualuav2026}, RF-EH/SWIPT operation~\cite{perera2018swipt, pandey2025rfeh}, or UAV-enabled ISAC sensing~\cite{zhang2023drlisac}, but not all of these aspects under a single, constraint-aware dual-UAV formulation. Several works also simplify the propulsion energy or omit information-causality and collision-avoidance constraints, which can make the resulting policy infeasible for practical relay/jammer flights. Hence, the present work focuses on a more realistic model that jointly captures A2G/G2A fading, secrecy degradation due to Eve, cooperative jamming, Cramér Rao Bound based sensing quality, and aerodynamic energy consumption.

This motivates a formulation in which the relay trajectory, jammer trajectory, communication resources, sensing time, AN/jamming power, and battery evolution are optimized together rather than independently. The objective is not only to improve the learned utility, but also to maintain physically meaningful $U_R$/$U_J$ separation, feasible battery dynamics, and well-defined secrecy and sensing metrics throughout the mission.

\section{System Model}
\label{sec:system}

\subsection{System Overview and Design Objectives}

We consider a dual-UAV secure communication and sensing scenario in which a terrestrial base station B communicates with a legitimate ground destination or target node D through a relay UAV $U_R$, while a second UAV $U_J$ transmits cooperative jamming signals to suppress a passive eavesdropper. A wireless power-transfer phase is also considered so that the aerial relay and jammer operation remains consistent with RF-energy-harvesting dual-UAV designs~\cite{pandeydualuav2026, pandey2025rfeh}. The same dual-UAV deployment is also well suited to integrated sensing and communication because the relay-jammer geometry simultaneously determines the quality of the legitimate communication path, the interference imposed on the wiretap link, and the bistatic sensing geometry seen by the target. As a result, trajectory design, sensing quality, and secure information delivery are coupled through the same UAV motion variables instead of being optimized independently.

From an integrated sensing and communication perspective, the dual-UAV structure is attractive because it allows communication support and sensing support to be coordinated over the same spectrum, time resources, and spatial geometry. The relay UAV maintains a strong information path between the base station and destination, while the second UAV improves spatial diversity, shapes the bistatic angle for sensing, and weakens the eavesdropper channel through cooperative jamming~\cite{wang2020energysecure, wang2021robust3d, zhang2023drlisac}. This joint operation is especially relevant when the destination also acts as the sensing target, since the same geometry that improves localization accuracy can also improve the secrecy-aware communication link. The dual-UAV setting therefore provides a natural platform for integrated sensing and communication with realistic mobility, battery, and separation constraints.

\subsection{Deployment Scenario}
The fixed ground nodes are denoted by $\mathbf{w}_B$, $\mathbf{w}_D$, and $\mathbf{w}_E$ for the base station, legitimate destination or target, and Eve, respectively. The horizontal trajectory of UAV $\varrho \in \{R,J\}$ in slot $t$ is denoted by $\mathbf{q}_{\varrho}(t)=[x_{\varrho}(t),y_{\varrho}(t)]$, and its altitude is denoted by $H_{\varrho}(t)$. The UAVs operate in a bounded region $\mathcal{A}$, satisfy $H_{\min}\leq H_{\varrho}(t)\leq H_{\max}$, and move between adjacent slots with maximum speed $v_{\max}$. Each timestep is $\Delta t$, and the operation lasts up to $T_{\max}$ steps or until the battery of either UAV is depleted.

During the WPT portion of a slot, the BS transfers RF energy that can be used by $U_R$ and $U_J$ for relaying and jamming, while propulsion and flight-control energy are drawn from onboard batteries as commonly assumed in RF-EH UAV studies~\cite{pandeyswiptjam2025, pandeydualuav2026}. During the information-transfer portion, $U_R$ forwards the BS signal to D and $U_J$ emits a cooperative jamming signal. Eve may intercept both the B--$U_R$ and $U_R$--D phases, while $U_J$ attempts to reduce Eve's received SINR. For the ISAC component, D is also treated as the sensing target; hence, the UAV geometry affects both the secure communication path and the CRB-based sensing metric.

\subsection{Rician Fading Channel Model}
Unlike free-space path loss assumptions, we adopt a comprehensive air-to-ground channel model that incorporates Rician fading, LoS/NLoS transitions, and log-normal shadowing, which is critical for accurate UAV communication modeling~\cite{khuwaja2018survey, pandey2025rfeh, pandeyiot2023}. This channel model captures the unique characteristics of UAV-to-ground links, including elevation-angle-dependent propagation and the dominance of LoS components in aerial communications~\cite{pandeydualuav2026}.

\subsubsection{Path Loss}
The distance-dependent path loss (in dB) follows the log-normal distance model commonly adopted for UAV communications~\cite{khuwaja2018survey, pandeyiot2023}:
\begin{equation}
\text{PL}(d) = \beta_0 + 10 \alpha_{\text{PL}} \log_{10}(d) + \xi,
\label{eq:pathloss}
\end{equation}
where $\beta_0 = 20\log_{10}(4\pi f_c / c)$~(dB) is the free-space reference path loss at 1~m, with $f_c$ denoting the carrier frequency and $c$ the speed of light; $\alpha_{\text{PL}}$ is the environment dependent path loss exponent for LoS and NLoS conditions; and $\xi \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_{\xi}^2)$ models log-normal shadowing~\cite{pandeyiot2023, pandeyrfehiot2025}.

\subsubsection{LoS Probability}
The probability of LoS link depends on elevation angle $\theta$ (in degrees) and is modeled according to the ITU-R framework as adopted in UAV communication literature~\cite{khuwaja2018survey, pandeydualuav2026}:
\begin{equation}
P_{\text{LoS}}(\theta) = \frac{1}{1 + a \exp(-b(\theta - a))},
\label{eq:los}
\end{equation}
where parameters $(a, b)$ are environment dependent coefficients whose values are listed in Tables~\ref{tab:sim_params_a} and~\ref{tab:sim_params_b}.

\subsubsection{Rician Fading}
The channel gain $|h|^2$ follows a Rician distribution that accounts for both LoS and multipath components~\cite{khuwaja2018survey, pandeyiot2023}. Let $h_r, h_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$ represent the scattered components. The channel gain is given by:
\begin{equation}
|h|^2 = \left| \sqrt{\frac{K}{K+1}} + \sqrt{\frac{1}{2(K+1)}}(h_r + jh_i) \right|^2,
\label{eq:rician}
\end{equation}
where $K$ is the Rician K-factor in linear scale for the respective LoS and NLoS components.

\subsection{CRB-Based Bistatic Sensing}
We adopt a bistatic sensing model in which UAV-1, represented by $U_R$, transmits sensing waveforms toward D and UAV-2, represented by $U_J$, can act as the spatially separated receiving platform. Let $D_{\text{UAV1},D}$ denote the UAV-1-to-D distance and $D_{\text{UAV2},D}$ denote the D-to-UAV-2 distance.

\subsubsection{Bistatic Radar Equation}
The sensing SNR at target location $\mathbf{p}_T$ is given by the bistatic radar equation~\cite{zhang2023drlisac}:
\begin{equation}
\gamma_{\text{radar}} = \frac{P_t G_t G_r \lambda^2 \sigma}{(4\pi)^3 D_{\text{UAV1},D}^2 D_{\text{UAV2},D}^2 k T_0 B F},
\label{eq:radar_snr}
\end{equation}
where $P_t = \tau_{\text{sense}} \cdot P_{\text{tx}}$ is the allocated sensing power; $G_t$ and $G_r$ are the transmit and receive antenna gains; $\lambda = c / f_s$ is the wavelength at sensing frequency $f_s$; $\sigma$ is the target radar cross-section (RCS); $B$ is the sensing bandwidth; $F$ is the receiver noise figure; and $kT_0$ is the thermal noise power density. All parameter values are listed in Tables~\ref{tab:sim_params_a} and~\ref{tab:sim_params_b}.

\subsubsection{Geometric Dilution of Precision (GDOP)}
For bistatic localization, GDOP measures how the UAV geometry affects localization accuracy~\cite{zhang2023drlisac}:
\begin{equation}
\text{GDOP} = \frac{1}{|\sin(\angle(U_R,D,U_J))| + \epsilon},
\label{eq:gdop}
\end{equation}
where $\angle(U_R,D,U_J)$ is the bistatic angle formed by the relay UAV, destination/target D, and jammer UAV, and $\epsilon$ is a small positive regularizer that prevents singularities. Optimal GDOP occurs at $90^\circ$ separation.

\subsubsection{Cram\'{e}r-Rao Bound}
The Cram\'{e}r-Rao bound provides both lower and upper bounds on the position estimation error. The CRB lower bound (CRLB) represents the minimum achievable mean-squared error (MSE) of any unbiased estimator, and is given by~\cite{zhang2023drlisac}:
\begin{equation}
\text{CRLB} = \frac{c}{2B} \cdot \frac{\text{GDOP}}{\sqrt{\gamma_{\text{radar}}}},
\label{eq:crb}
\end{equation}
where $c / 2B$ is the range resolution cell. This serves as the lower bound: no unbiased estimator can achieve RMSE below $\sqrt{\text{CRLB}}$. An upper bound on the estimation error can be approximated using the worst-case GDOP (i.e., when the bistatic angle approaches $0^\circ$ or $180^\circ$):
\begin{equation}
\text{CRB}_{\text{upper}} = \frac{c}{2B} \cdot \frac{\text{GDOP}_{\max}}{\sqrt{\gamma_{\text{radar}}}}, \quad \text{GDOP}_{\max} = \frac{1}{\epsilon},
\label{eq:crb_upper}
\end{equation}
where $\text{GDOP}_{\max} = 1/\epsilon$ corresponds to the degenerate collinear configuration. The sensing utility maps the CRB to a reward signal:
\begin{equation}
U_{\text{sense}} = \tanh\left(\frac{\kappa_{\text{CRB}}}{\text{CRLB}}\right) \cdot \tau_{\text{sense}},
\label{eq:sense_util}
\end{equation}
where $\kappa_{\text{CRB}}$ is a scaling constant used to normalize the CRLB-driven sensing reward. This mapping assigns a higher sensing utility to lower CRLB values, i.e., better localization accuracy.

\subsection{Wireless Information Transfer and Secrecy Rate}
We now describe the two-phase information-transfer (IT) model for the relay/jammer topology, following the dual-UAV secure IoT formulation in~\cite{pandeydualuav2026} and the cooperative-jamming secrecy model in~\cite{pandeyswiptjam2025}. In the first IT phase, B transmits the confidential symbol $u_B[n]$ to $U_R$, while $U_J$ transmits the unit-energy jamming symbol $u_J[n]$ to degrade Eve's wiretap channel. The signal received at node $o\in\{R,E_k\}$ is
\begin{equation}
z_{B,o}[n]=\sqrt{P_B g_{B,o}[n]}u_B[n]+\sqrt{P_J[n]g_{J,o}[n]}u_J[n]+n_o[n],
\label{eq:rx_phase1}
\end{equation}
where $P_B$ is the BS transmit power, $P_J[n]$ is the jammer transmit power, $g_{i,j}[n]$ denotes the channel power gain for link $i$--$j$, and $n_o[n]\sim\mathcal{CN}(0,N_o)$ is additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN).

In the second IT phase, $U_R$ decodes the information received from B and forwards the unit-energy relay symbol $u_R[n]$ to D, while $U_J$ continues transmitting jamming signals toward Eve. The received signal at $l\in\{D,E_k\}$ is modeled as~\cite{pandeydualuav2026}
\begin{equation}
z_{R,l}[n]=\sqrt{P_R[n]g_{R,l}[n]}u_R[n]+\sqrt{P_J[n]g_{J,l}[n]}u_J[n]+n_l[n],
\label{eq:rx_phase2}
\end{equation}
where $P_R[n]$ is the relay transmit power and $n_l[n]\sim\mathcal{CN}(0,N_l)$ denotes AWGN at target(D) or Eavesdropper($E_k$). Similar to~\cite{pandeydualuav2026}, we assume that $U_R$ and D can remove the known cooperative-jamming signal from their received observations, whereas Eve cannot cancel the jamming signal while decoding the confidential information.

\subsubsection{Instantaneous SNR and Achievable Rate}
The received SNR is derived from the ratio of average received signal power to noise power. Let $P_{\text{tx}}$ be the transmitted power and $N_0$ the noise power spectral density times bandwidth. The received signal power at distance $d$ is $P_{\text{rx}} = P_{\text{tx}} \cdot |h|^2 / \text{PL}_{\text{lin}}(d)$, where $\text{PL}_{\text{lin}} = 10^{\text{PL}_{\text{dB}}/10}$ is the linear-scale path loss from~\eqref{eq:pathloss}. The instantaneous SNR is then~\cite{pandeyiot2023, pandeyrfehiot2025}
\begin{equation}
\gamma(d, \theta) = \frac{P_{\text{rx}}}{N_0} = \frac{P_{\text{tx}} \cdot |h|^2}{\text{PL}_{\text{lin}}(d) \cdot N_0}.
\label{eq:snr}
\end{equation}
The link SNRs $\gamma_{B,R}$, $\gamma_{R,D}$, $\gamma_{B,E}$, and $\gamma_{R,E}$ are obtained by applying~\eqref{eq:snr} to the corresponding B--$U_R$, $U_R$--D, B--Eve, and $U_R$--Eve distances. For the multi-user setting, the achievable rate of user $n$ assigned to UAV $i$ is
\begin{equation}
R_n = \log_2(1 + \gamma_n) \cdot (1 - \tau_{\text{sense}}) \cdot (1 - p_{\text{AN}}),
\label{eq:rate}
\end{equation}
where $\gamma_n$ is the corresponding received SNR, $\tau_{\text{sense}} \in [0,1]$ is the time fraction allocated to sensing, and $p_{\text{AN}} \in [0,1]$ is the power fraction for artificial noise~\cite{pandeyswiptjam2025}.

Because the small-scale fading terms vary across slots, achievable rates are expressed as expectations over channel realizations~\cite{pandeydualuav2026}. Let $\alpha_{\text{TS}}[n]$ denote the time-switching ratio used for WPT, so the remaining IT duration is $(1-\alpha_{\text{TS}}[n])\Delta t$ and the two-hop relay transmission uses a factor $1/2$. The legitimate B--D link rate is
\begin{equation}
R_l[n]=\mathbb{E}\left[\frac{(1-\alpha_{\text{TS}}[n])\Delta t}{2}\log_2\left(1+\min(\gamma_{B,R}[n],\gamma_{R,D}[n])\right)\right],
\label{eq:legitimate_rate}
\end{equation}
where $\gamma_{B,R}[n]=P_Bg_{B,R}[n]/N_R$ and $\gamma_{R,D}[n]=P_R[n]g_{R,D}[n]/N_D$ denote the SNRs of the B--$U_R$ and $U_R$--D links, respectively. The relay information-causality condition is written as $\sum_{n=1}^{m-1}R_{B,R}[n]\geq\sum_{n=2}^{m}R_{R,D}[n]$ for $m\in\{2,\ldots,N\}$, where $R_{B,R}[n]$ and $R_{R,D}[n]$ are obtained from the same Shannon-rate form using $\gamma_{B,R}[n]$ and $\gamma_{R,D}[n]$.

For the worst-case colluding-eavesdropper scenario, Eve can combine information leaked from both IT phases. The eavesdropping rate is therefore~\cite{pandeydualuav2026}
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
R_w[n]
&=\mathbb{E}\left[\frac{(1-\alpha_{\text{TS}}[n])\Delta t}{2}
\log_2\left(1+\Gamma_E[n]\right)\right],\\
\Gamma_E[n]
&=\sum_{E_k\in\Phi_e}\left(\gamma_{B,E_k}[n]+\gamma_{R,E_k}[n]\right),
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:eaves_rate}
\end{equation}
where $\Phi_e$ is the eavesdropper point process. The corresponding SINRs are $\gamma_{B,E_k}[n]=P_Bg_{B,E_k}[n]/(P_J[n]g_{J,E_k}[n]+N_{E_k})$ and $\gamma_{R,E_k}[n]=P_R[n]g_{R,E_k}[n]/(P_J[n]g_{J,E_k}[n]+N_{E_k})$, which explicitly show the interference created by $U_J$.

Thus, the instantaneous secrecy rate is given by the positive difference between the legitimate and wiretap rates~\cite{pandey2022security, pandeydualuav2026}:
\begin{equation}
R_s[n]=[R_l[n]-R_w[n]]^+,\quad [x]^+=\max(x,0).
\label{eq:secrecy}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Aerodynamic UAV Energy Model}
Unlike linear approximations used in simplified UAV models, we adopt the rotary-wing propulsion model used in recent UAV trajectory studies~\cite{zeng2019energy, pandeydualuav2026, pandeyaoidrl2025}. For UAV $\varrho\in\{R,J\}$ with horizontal speed $v_{\varrho}(t)$, the flight propulsion power is
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
P_{\varrho,f}(t)={}&P_0\left(1+\frac{3v_{\varrho}^2(t)}{U_{\text{tip}}^2}\right)
+P_i\left(\sqrt{1+\frac{v_{\varrho}^4(t)}{4v_0^4}}-\frac{v_{\varrho}^2(t)}{2v_0^2}\right)^{\frac{1}{2}} \\
&+\frac{1}{2}d_0\rho sA v_{\varrho}^3(t)+m g [v_{\varrho,z}(t)]^+ ,
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:power}
\end{equation}
where $P_{\varrho,f}(t)$ is the flight propulsion power of UAV $\varrho$, $P_0$ and $P_i$ denote the blade-profile power and induced hover power, respectively, $U_{\text{tip}}$ is the rotor blade tip speed, $v_0$ is the mean rotor induced velocity in hover, $d_0$ is the fuselage drag ratio, $\rho$ is the air density, $s$ is the rotor solidity, $A$ is the rotor disc area, $v_{\varrho}(t)$ is the horizontal speed, $m$ is the UAV mass, $g$ is the gravitational acceleration, and $[v_{\varrho,z}(t)]^+=\max(v_{\varrho,z}(t),0)$ keeps only the positive vertical climbing velocity. The total slot power can be written as $P_{\varrho,\text{total}}(t)=P_{\varrho,f}(t)+P_{\varrho,\text{tx}}(t)+P_{\varrho,\text{sense}}(t)\approx P_{\varrho,f}(t)$ because the communication and sensing transmission powers are much smaller than the propulsion power of a rotary-wing UAV in normal operation.

\subsubsection{Battery Dynamics}
Energy consumed per step (normalized) follows the battery discharge accounting used in UAV optimization literature~\cite{pandeydualuav2026, pandeyaoidrl2025}:
\begin{equation}
\Delta E_{\varrho}(t) = \frac{P_{\varrho,\text{total}}(t)\Delta t}{3600 E_{\text{battery}}},
\label{eq:battery}
\end{equation}
where $E_{\text{battery}}$ is the UAV battery capacity in Wh. The value 3600 appears because $1~\text{Wh}=3600~\text{J}$; therefore $P_{\varrho,\text{total}}(t)\Delta t$ in Joules is divided by $3600E_{\text{battery}}$ to obtain the normalized fraction of Wh battery energy consumed during the slot.

\subsection{Queue-Theoretic QoS Model}
Users experience stochastic packet arrivals with rate $\lambda_q$. The queue evolution follows the standard discrete-time queue dynamics $Q_n(t+1)=\max(0,Q_n(t)+\lambda_q\Delta t-\mu_n(t)\Delta t)$, with $\mu_n(t)=\log_2(1+\gamma_n(t))$ representing the Shannon service rate. The QoS metric is computed as $\text{QoS}_n=1-Q_n/Q_{\max}$, providing a normalized measure of service quality without assigning a separate equation number to these auxiliary update rules.

\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:problem}

The joint design problem is formulated over the relay trajectory $\mathbf{q}_R(t)$, jammer trajectory $\mathbf{q}_J(t)$, WPT/IT time-switching ratio $\alpha_{\text{TS}}(t)$, sensing-time fraction $\tau_{\text{sense}}(t)$, AN/jamming power fraction $p_{\text{AN}}(t)$, and UAV transmit powers $P_{\varrho,\text{tx}}(t)$ for $\varrho\in\{R,J\}$. Let $\mathcal{X}$ denote this set of optimization variables. The long-term objective maximizes a discounted weighted sum of performance utilities, where the instantaneous utility $U_t$ combines communication, sensing, secrecy, and energy terms. The discount factor is denoted by $\gamma_{\text{d}}\in(0,1]$ to distinguish it from the SNR variables $\gamma_n$, $\gamma_{\text{user}}$, $\gamma_{\text{eve}}$, and $\gamma_{\text{radar}}$ defined earlier. To remain consistent with the normalized time-state representation, the discounting term is written with the normalized slot index $t/T_{\max}\in[1/T_{\max},1]$. Inspired by the constrained dual-UAV formulation in~\cite{pandeydualuav2026}, the optimization problem is written as
\begin{align}
(\text{P1}):\quad
\max_{\mathcal{X}} \quad
& \mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{t=1}^{T_{\max}} \gamma_{\text{d}}^{t/T_{\max}} U_t\right] \label{eq:problem}\\
\text{s.t.}\quad
& 0 \leq \alpha_{\text{TS}}(t) \leq 1,\quad
  0 \leq \tau_{\text{sense}}(t) \leq 1, \notag\\
& 0 \leq p_{\text{AN}}(t) \leq 1, \label{eq:c_tsr}\\
& 0 \leq P_{\varrho,\text{tx}}(t) \leq P_{\max}, \label{eq:c_power}\\
& E_{\varrho}(t+1)=E_{\varrho}(t)-\Delta E_{\varrho}(t), \notag\\
& E_{\varrho}(t)\geq 0, \label{eq:c_battery}\\
& \sum_{\ell=0}^{t}E_{\varrho}^{\text{harv}}(\ell)
  \geq
  \sum_{\ell=0}^{t}P_{\varrho,\text{tx}}(\ell)\Delta t, \label{eq:c_energy_causality}\\
& \sum_{\ell=0}^{t-1}R_{B,R}(\ell)
  \geq
  \sum_{\ell=1}^{t}R_{R,D}(\ell), \label{eq:c_info_causality}\\
& \|\mathbf{q}_{\varrho}(t+1)-\mathbf{q}_{\varrho}(t)\|
  \leq v_{\max}\Delta t, \label{eq:c_speed}\\
& H_{\min} \leq H_{\varrho}(t) \leq H_{\max}, \label{eq:c_altitude}\\
& \|\mathbf{q}_{R}(t)-\mathbf{q}_{J}(t)\| \geq D_{\min}, \label{eq:c_collision}\\
& \mathbf{q}_{\varrho}(t)\in\mathcal{A}, \label{eq:c_area}\\
& R_s(t) \geq R_s^{\min}. \label{eq:c_secrecy}
\end{align}
Here, $U_t$ is the weighted utility defined later in~\eqref{eq:reward}, $\ell$ is the summation index over previous slots, $E_{\varrho}^{\text{harv}}(\ell)$ is the RF energy harvested by UAV $\varrho$ in slot $\ell$, and $\mathcal{A}$ is the feasible horizontal flight region. Constraints are applied for all valid slots $t$ and, where UAV-specific terms appear, for $\varrho\in\{R,J\}$. Constraints~\eqref{eq:c_tsr}--\eqref{eq:c_power} bound the resource-allocation variables and power budget; \eqref{eq:c_battery} and \eqref{eq:c_energy_causality} impose battery and RF-harvested energy causality; \eqref{eq:c_info_causality} follows the relay causality condition introduced after~\eqref{eq:legitimate_rate} and ensures that the relay cannot forward more information to D than it has decoded from the BS; \eqref{eq:c_speed}--\eqref{eq:c_area} impose UAV mobility, altitude, collision-avoidance, and area constraints; and \eqref{eq:c_secrecy} enforces a minimum secrecy-rate requirement based on~\eqref{eq:secrecy}. This problem is non-convex because the trajectories, channel gains, harvested energy, and secrecy terms are mutually coupled across time slots.

\section{Deep Reinforcement Learning Framework}
\label{sec:drl}

\subsection{Markov Decision Process Formulation}
The joint optimization problem is cast as a Markov Decision Process (MDP) with continuous state and action spaces to enable systematic learning of optimal UAV control policies.

\subsubsection{State Space}
The state $\mathbf{s}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{d_s}$ contains UAV positions $\{\mathbf{p}_{i}\}_{i=1}^2 \in \mathbb{R}^3$, UAV headings $\{\psi_i\}_{i=1}^2 \in [-\pi, \pi]$, battery levels $\{B_i\}_{i=1}^2 \in [0, 1]$, user positions $\{\mathbf{u}_n\}_{n=1}^N \in \mathbb{R}^2$, user queue lengths $\{Q_n\}_{n=1}^N \in [0, Q_{\max}]$, target positions $\{\mathbf{p}_t\}_{t=1}^T \in \mathbb{R}^2$, target confidence $\{c_t\}_{t=1}^T \in [0, 1]$, and the normalized remaining-time state $t_{\mathrm{rem}}=(T_{\max}-t+1)/T_{\max}\in[1/T_{\max},1]$. All states are normalized to $[-1, 1]$ for stable learning.

\subsubsection{Action Space}
For each UAV, a 5-D continuous action $\mathbf{a}_i \in [-1, 1]^5$:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{a}_i = [\Delta \psi_i,\, v_i,\, \Delta z_i,\, \tau_{\text{sense}, i},\, p_{\text{AN}, i}],
\label{eq:action}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta \psi_i \in [-30^\circ, 30^\circ]$ is the heading change, $v_i \in [0, v_{\max}]$ is horizontal speed, $\Delta z_i \in [-5, 5]~\si{m/s}$ is vertical velocity, $\tau_{\text{sense}, i} \in [0, 1]$ is time split for sensing, and $p_{\text{AN}, i} \in [0, 1]$ is power split for artificial noise. Total action: $\mathbf{a} = [\mathbf{a}_1; \mathbf{a}_2] \in \mathbb{R}^{10}$.

\subsubsection{Utility Function}
The instantaneous utility is defined as a weighted sum of communication, sensing, secrecy, and energy-related performance terms while also encouraging proper UAV separation:
\begin{equation}
U_t = \alpha R_{\text{comm}} + (1-\alpha) U_{\text{sense}} - \lambda_E U_{\text{energy}} - \eta U_{\text{leak}} - \mu P_{\text{safety}} + \beta D_{\text{UAV}},
\label{eq:reward}
\end{equation}
where $R_{\text{comm}}$ is the communication sum-rate computed from the legitimate-link rates in~\eqref{eq:legitimate_rate}, $U_{\text{sense}}$ is the sensing utility in~\eqref{eq:sense_util}, $U_{\text{energy}}$ is the normalized energy term obtained from~\eqref{eq:power}, and $U_{\text{sec}}=\tanh(R_s[n]/2)$ is the bounded secrecy utility derived from~\eqref{eq:secrecy}. The secrecy leakage term is written as $U_{\text{leak}} = 1 - U_{\text{sec}}$. In addition, $P_{\text{safety}}$ imposes penalties for separation less than $D_{\min}$ and for leaving the feasible region, while $D_{\text{UAV}} = \tanh(\|\mathbf{q}_R - \mathbf{q}_J\|/D_{\max})$ is the inter-UAV distance utility that increases with the separation between $U_R$ and $U_J$. The weighting coefficients $\alpha$, $\lambda_E$, $\eta$, $\mu$, and $\beta$ balance the individual utility components and their values are reported in Tables~\ref{tab:sim_params_a} and~\ref{tab:sim_params_b}.

\subsubsection{State Transition}
The state transition follows the environment dynamics. Given state $\mathbf{s}_t$ and action $\mathbf{a}_t$, the next state $\mathbf{s}_{t+1}$ is determined by the UAV kinematic update, battery depletion from~\eqref{eq:battery}, queue evolution described in the QoS model, and channel realizations drawn from the Rician fading model. The transition probability $\mathcal{P}(\mathbf{s}_{t+1} | \mathbf{s}_t, \mathbf{a}_t)$ is implicitly defined by these stochastic dynamics:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{P}(\mathbf{s}_{t+1} | \mathbf{s}_t, \mathbf{a}_t) = \prod_{n=1}^{N} p(\xi_n) \cdot \prod_{\ell\in\mathcal{L}} p(h_{\ell}),
\label{eq:transition}
\end{equation}
where $p(\xi_n)$ is the shadowing distribution $\xi_n \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_\xi^2)$ from~\eqref{eq:pathloss}, $\mathcal{L}$ is the set of active wireless links, and $p(h_{\ell})$ is the Rician fading distribution of link $\ell$ from~\eqref{eq:rician}. All deterministic state components (UAV positions, battery levels, queue lengths) evolve according to fixed dynamics conditioned on the action.

\subsection{Soft Actor-Critic Algorithm}
SAC~\cite{haarnoja2018sac} is adopted because the dual-UAV relay/jammer problem has continuous actions, stochastic Rician channels, coupled energy and secrecy constraints, and a non-convex reward surface. Similar to the DRL-based UAV formulations in~\cite{pandeyaoidrl2025, pandeystarris2026, pandeydualuav2026}, SAC learns a stochastic policy from interaction data rather than requiring a closed-form solution of (P1) at each time slot. The key advantage of SAC is its maximum-entropy objective: the agent is rewarded not only for high return, but also for maintaining sufficient action diversity during training. This is useful in the present setting because the relay and jammer must explore different 3D geometries before converging to stable B--$U_R$--D communication and $U_J$--Eve jamming behavior.

The policy, or actor, is denoted by $\pi_\theta(\mathbf{a}_t|\mathbf{s}_t)$, where $\theta$ represents the actor-network parameters. For a given state $\mathbf{s}_t$, the actor outputs a Gaussian mean and log-standard deviation, i.e., $\mu_\theta(\mathbf{s}_t)$ and $\log\sigma_\theta(\mathbf{s}_t)$. A raw action is sampled using the reparameterization trick as $\tilde{\mathbf{a}}_t=\mu_\theta(\mathbf{s}_t)+\sigma_\theta(\mathbf{s}_t)\odot\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_t$, where $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_t\sim\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\mathbf{I})$. The final action is obtained by applying a $\tanh(\cdot)$ squashing function and then mapping each component to its feasible interval. This mapping gives the heading variation, horizontal speed, altitude change, sensing-time fraction, and AN/jamming power split for both UAVs while respecting the bounds in~\eqref{eq:c_tsr} and~\eqref{eq:c_speed}.

Two soft Q-functions, $Q_{\phi_1}$ and $Q_{\phi_2}$, are used to reduce positive bias in value estimation. For a mini-batch transition $(\mathbf{s}_t,\mathbf{a}_t,U_t,\mathbf{s}_{t+1})$ drawn from replay buffer $\mathcal{D}$, the target value is
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
y_t={}&U_t+\gamma_{\text{d}}\Big[
\min_{i=1,2}Q_{\bar{\phi}_i}(\mathbf{s}_{t+1},\mathbf{a}_{t+1})\\
&-\alpha_{\text{ent}}\log\pi_\theta(\mathbf{a}_{t+1}|\mathbf{s}_{t+1})\Big],
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:sac_target}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{a}_{t+1}\sim\pi_\theta(\cdot|\mathbf{s}_{t+1})$, $\bar{\phi}_i$ are target-network parameters, $\gamma_{\text{d}}\in(0,1]$ is the discount factor defined in (P1), and $\alpha_{\text{ent}}$ is the entropy-temperature coefficient. The critic loss is then
\begin{equation}
J_Q(\phi_i)=\mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{D}}\left[\left(Q_{\phi_i}(\mathbf{s}_t,\mathbf{a}_t)-y_t\right)^2\right],\quad i\in\{1,2\}.
\label{eq:sac_critic_loss}
\end{equation}

The actor is updated by maximizing the entropy-regularized value, equivalently minimizing
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
J_{\pi}(\theta)=\mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{s}_t\sim\mathcal{D}}\Big[
&\alpha_{\text{ent}}\log\pi_\theta(\mathbf{a}_t|\mathbf{s}_t)\\
&-\min_{i=1,2}Q_{\phi_i}(\mathbf{s}_t,\mathbf{a}_t)
\Big],
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:sac_actor_loss}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{a}_t$ is sampled from the current policy using the reparameterized action. The entropy temperature is automatically adjusted toward a target entropy $\bar{\mathcal{H}}$ by minimizing
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
J(\alpha_{\text{ent}})=
\mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{a}_t\sim\pi_\theta}\Big[
&-\alpha_{\text{ent}}\big(\log\pi_\theta(\mathbf{a}_t|\mathbf{s}_t)\\
&+\bar{\mathcal{H}}\big)\Big].
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:sac_temp_loss}
\end{equation}
After each gradient update, the target critics are softly updated as $\bar{\phi}_i\leftarrow \tau\phi_i+(1-\tau)\bar{\phi}_i$, where $\tau\ll1$ is the target-smoothing coefficient.

\begin{algorithm}[!t]
\caption{SAC-Based Dual-UAV Relay/Jammer Optimization}
\label{alg:sac}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE Initialize actor $\pi_\theta$, critics $Q_{\phi_1},Q_{\phi_2}$, target critics $Q_{\bar{\phi}_1},Q_{\bar{\phi}_2}$, replay buffer $\mathcal{D}$, and temperature $\alpha_{\text{ent}}$.
\FOR{each episode}
\STATE Reset UAV positions, batteries, channels, queue states, and Eve realization.
\FOR{each time slot $t$}
\STATE Observe $\mathbf{s}_t$ and sample action $\mathbf{a}_t\sim\pi_\theta(\cdot|\mathbf{s}_t)$.
\STATE Map $\mathbf{a}_t$ to UAV motion, $\alpha_{\text{TS}}(t)$, $\tau_{\text{sense}}(t)$, and $p_{\text{AN}}(t)$.
\STATE Evaluate channel gains, $R_l(t)$, $R_w(t)$, $R_s(t)$, sensing utility, and energy consumption.
\STATE Compute utility $U_t$ and apply penalties for violated constraints in~\eqref{eq:c_battery}--\eqref{eq:c_secrecy}.
\STATE Store $(\mathbf{s}_t,\mathbf{a}_t,U_t,\mathbf{s}_{t+1})$ in $\mathcal{D}$.
\STATE Sample a mini-batch from $\mathcal{D}$ and update critics using~\eqref{eq:sac_critic_loss}.
\STATE Update actor using~\eqref{eq:sac_actor_loss} and update $\alpha_{\text{ent}}$ using~\eqref{eq:sac_temp_loss}.
\STATE Soft-update target critics.
\ENDFOR
\ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presented a comprehensive dual-UAV ISAC framework integrating advanced physical layer models with deep reinforcement learning. By incorporating Rician fading channels~\cite{khuwaja2018survey}, CRB-based bistatic sensing~\cite{zhang2023drlisac}, secrecy-rate modeling with cooperative jamming~\cite{pandeyswiptjam2025, pandeydualuav2026}, and aerodynamic energy consumption~\cite{zeng2019energy, pandeyaoidrl2025}, the proposed approach demonstrates strong energy efficiency gains (up to 22\% improvement over competitive baselines) and establishes a highly adaptable trade-off front across communication, sensing, and security metrics. The SAC agent successfully learns to differentiate relay and jammer/sensing roles, demonstrating the effectiveness of DRL for multi-objective UAV-ISAC optimization under realistic physical constraints.

Several promising directions remain for future investigation. First, the framework can be extended to multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) with decentralized execution for swarms of three or more UAVs, as explored in~\cite{lyu2023madrl}. Second, the integration of reconfigurable intelligent surfaces (RIS) or STAR-RIS~\cite{pandeystarris2026} can provide additional degrees of freedom for channel manipulation and enhanced secrecy. Third, incorporating RF energy harvesting~\cite{pandey2025rfeh, pandeyaoidrl2025} would enable self-sustaining UAV operations for extended missions. Fourth, considering hardware imperfections~\cite{pandeyiot2023, pandeyrfehiot2025} in the channel and transceiver models would further bridge the gap between simulation and practical deployment. Finally, adversarial eavesdropper models with learning capabilities would necessitate robust policy training approaches.
